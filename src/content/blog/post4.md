I spent half a day reading _The Vegetarian_ by _Han Kang_, this year's Nobel Prize in Literature winner. The novel is packed with raw and potent imagery, telling the story of a woman who rebels against human society by becoming a vegetarian and ultimately transforming into a plant.

The story unfolds from three distinct perspectives. The first part is told from the viewpoint of her husband, a conventional, dull, indifferent man who exploits her labor within their marriage. On the day before she decides to stop eating meat, she cuts her hand while preparing food for him. Instead of showing concern, he merely shouts at her impatiently. She gazes at the blood on her hand, and from that day, she starts having nightmares about meat and decides to become a vegetarian. This moment may symbolize her sudden awakening—her realization that her body, too, is part of what has been taken from her.

The second male figure is her father, an authoritarian patriarch typical of East Asian families, who forces his daughter to eat meat despite her refusal, ultimately driving her to attempt suicide by slitting her wrists. Through memories woven into the narrative, we glimpse her childhood trauma: the father’s brutal killing of her pet dog, followed by the family consuming its meat. He also subjects both her and her sister to domestic violence.

The second part of the novel shifts to her brother-in-law’s perspective, a seemingly different male figure—an artist. Yet, this part of the story symbolizes the male gaze, revealing how even a man who fancies himself an artist can objectify women, turning them into mere objects of desire, which eventually leads to sexual violence.

The third section is told through the voice of her sister, who initially cannot understand her sibling’s behavior. But through reflection and memory, she comes to realize that she has been a silent accomplice all along. Mothers or elder women in patriarchal societies are often not only victims but also complicit enforcers of the same oppressive system. After her sister’s breakdown, she begins to see the cracks in her own life, the unhappiness she has endured in both marriage and womanhood. She regrets not intervening to save her sister, but she also recognizes how trapped she has been in her own life. When her sister declares her desire to become a plant, to stop eating altogether, and asks, “Why can’t I die?” the older sister is shocked. But in her own memories, she starts to reveal her own near-suicide attempts, slowly understanding that her sister had only seen the violence and futility of it all before she did. Eventually, she reaches a place of understanding and stops the doctors from force-feeding her sister.

She watches the trees on the roadside, like silent beasts, their burning branches a silent protest.

Here, the novel ends—a bleak and suffocating conclusion. A woman desperate to escape human society, who is seen by the system as abnormal, is continually pulled back to so-called normality. The societal order is voiced by her mother: “If you don’t eat meat, you’ll be eaten.” But the protagonist uses the autonomy of her own body to resist, choosing to transform into a plant. It’s a form of resistance, albeit self-destructive. Is it powerful or powerless? When a person has no way out, their body may be the only thing they can truly claim as their own. Han Kang once said, “When I write, I often think about these questions: How far can human violence go? Where do we draw the line between reason and madness? To what extent can we understand others? I hope The Vegetarian can answer these questions. Through it, I wanted to portray a woman who would rather die than join the human race.”

After finishing the novel, I immediately thought of a film I watched a few years ago: _Raw_ by _Julia Ducournau_, who became the second woman to win the Palme d’Or for her provocative film _Titane_. The story of _Raw_ could almost serve as a counterpoint to _The Vegetarian_, offering a fascinating contrast between the two depictions of female agency.

In _Raw_, we also meet two sisters raised in a vegetarian household. But when the younger sister enters university, she gradually discovers a desire to eat human flesh, a hunger that is both awakened and encouraged by her older sister.

_Ducournau_ wraps her ideas in this extreme, alienating narrative. Raw examines the boundaries between humanity and animality, the repression of desire, collective bullying, the existence of marginalized groups, and the thin line separating madness from civilization. One key figure in the film is the protagonist’s gay friend, representing a hidden minority in society. The film ultimately reveals that the protagonist’s craving for meat is inherited from her mother, while her father willingly accepts being consumed. The gay friend remarks how he hid his identity for over twenty years, just as the protagonist’s family hid their desires behind the label of vegetarianism. In the end, this hidden desire is laid bare, impossible to suppress. While eating animal meat is socially acceptable, can the existence of a cannibal ever be justified? Of course, we know that such a figure cannot be tolerated in modern society.

At its core, both Raw and The Vegetarian question the limits of human society, though from opposing extremes. In The Vegetarian, the protagonist resists human violence by becoming a plant, existing at the threshold between plant and human. In Raw, a body horror film filled with graphic violence and bloodshed, the protagonist inhabits the border between humanity and beast.

What often goes unnoticed in Raw is the gender symbolism embedded within it. The protagonist and her sister mimic men by standing on the rooftop and urinating like them. The older sister lures a man out of his car by pretending to be weak, only to kill and devour him—a reversal of predator and prey. These sisters possess animalistic instincts while performing within the constraints of human social structures, even gender norms. In the end, the older sister is locked away, further institutionalized—a deeper form of taming. This might echo Judith Butler’s theory of gender performativity. Raw presents an identity that defies categorization, a notion that Ducournau pushes even further in Titane, where the fusion of woman and machine transcends human identity to form a cyborg.

In Ducournau’s work, women are no longer mere victims or weak figures; it’s difficult even to say they conform to gender identities. Her stories are exploring non-normative desires and identities outside societal expectations. Han Kang, on the other hand, writes from a perspective closer to the lived realities of East Asian women, whose daily lives are still shaped by exploitation and oppression under a traditional patriarchal system. This contrast also reflects the difference between feminist agendas. For me, though, it is Han Kang’s narrative that resonates more deeply.
